11 Experiment

The key components of experimental research are:

Independent variable: The variable that the researcher deliberately changes in order to see its effect on another variable. Dependent variable: The variable that the researcher measures to see how it is affected by the independent variable. Control group: A group of subjects that is not exposed to the independent variable. This group is used to compare to the experimental group to see the effect of the independent variable. Experimental group: The group of subjects that is exposed to the independent variable. Randomization: The process of randomly assigning subjects to the control group or the experimental group. This helps to ensure that the groups are similar at the beginning of the experiment. Replication: Repeating the experiment multiple times with different groups of subjects. This helps to increase the accuracy of the results. In addition to these key components, experimental research also typically includes a hypothesis, a research question, a literature review, a methodology, and a discussion of the results.

Here is a more detailed explanation of each of the key components of experimental research:

Independent variable: The independent variable is the variable that the researcher deliberately changes in order to see its effect on another variable. It is important that the independent variable is the only thing that is different between the control group and the experimental group. This helps to ensure that any changes in the dependent variable are caused by the independent variable and not by other factors. Dependent variable: The dependent variable is the variable that the researcher measures to see how it is affected by the independent variable. It is important that the dependent variable is measurable and that it can be affected by the independent variable. Control group: The control group is a group of subjects that is not exposed to the independent variable. This group is used to compare to the experimental group to see the effect of the independent variable. The control group should be as similar as possible to the experimental group in all other respects, except for the independent variable. Experimental group: The experimental group is the group of subjects that is exposed to the independent variable. The experimental group should be as similar as possible to the control group in all other respects, except for the independent variable. Randomization: Randomization is the process of randomly assigning subjects to the control group or the experimental group. This helps to ensure that the groups are similar at the beginning of the experiment. Randomization can be done by using a random number generator or by drawing names out of a hat. Replication: Replication is repeating the experiment multiple times with different groups of subjects. This helps to increase the accuracy of the results. Replication can be done by repeating the experiment with the same participants or by repeating the experiment with different participants. Experimental research is a rigorous approach to studying the cause-and-effect relationship between two variables. By carefully controlling the variables in an experiment, researchers can be more confident that the results of their study are accurate.
